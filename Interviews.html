Quick note on these interviews: These were for the original idea of a phone lock box for studying.

Interviewee 1

Interviewee 1 is a young individual with a standard upbringing in this digital age. They got their first phone in 6th grade for safety and communication with their parents due to their school‚Äôs distance. Initially, the phone was used for games and music, but in high school, it evolved into a tool for interaction with peers. The shift increased their phone use significantly, creating a burden rather than a convenience.

Interviewee 1 describes themselves as addicted to their phone, defining addiction as the reflexive need to check it, even without a specific purpose. They spend 7-8 hours daily on their phone, texting, gaming, and listening to music. They enjoy the connection texting provides and acknowledges that some of it comes from obligation not genuine enjoyment. Their phone usage has mixed impacts on their relationships, at times enhancing interactions but often pulling them away from present moments. Efforts to limit their usage, such as physically distancing themselves from the phone, have had little success.

Insights

Interviewee 1 reflects on societal implications of phone addiction, especially among students. They perceive phones as major distractions in academic settings, often leading to autopilot scrolling on apps like Instagram. However, they also appreciate the potential of creative solutions that blend physical and digital barriers to mitigate overuse. Their ideal intervention would incorporate friction, such as requiring a physical action to unlock their phone.


Interviewee 2

Interviewee 3 is a professor in their mid-thirties to early fifties who has witnessed the evolution of mobile technology firsthand. They grew up without the pervasive presence of smartphones, which gives them a unique perspective on mobile device addiction. Their use of technology begun with a focus on practicality, such as checking email s or helping their children. However, over time, they have recognized patterns of overuse, particularly in the context of academic environments and personal routines.

The professor acknowledges their own phone addiction, defining it as a lack of control over their usage and a reflexive tendency to check the device. They typically spend 1.5 to 2 hours daily on their phone, allocated to emails, puzzles, and occasional social interactions. They value functional use but recognized that some scrolling and gaming habits are unnecessary. They are particularly interested in societal and educational implications of phone addiction and advocate for structural solutions rather than solely individual responsibility.

Interviewee 4

Interviewee 4 grew up with strict parental restrictions on technology use, which paradoxically fueled an increased reliance on phones and other devices as they gained independence. Over time, they came to view their phone as both a tool for productivity and a source of distraction. They acknowledge an addiction to screens but considers this addiction normalized due to its prevalence in modern society.

They observe that overusage of their phone has negatively impacted their academic focus and, to a lesser extent, their personal relationships. However, they also see value in approaching technology with intent while maintaining a balance between relaxation and productivity. They are comfortable with their current habits but recognizes a potential need to reassess their relationship with screens in the future.
